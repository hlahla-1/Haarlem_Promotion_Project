Teylers Museum


Teylers Museum is an art, natural history, and science museum in Haarlem, Netherlands. Established in 1778, Teylers Museum was founded as a centre for contemporary art and science. The historic centre of the museum is the neoclassical Oval Room, which was built behind the house of Pieter Teyler van der Hulst, the so-called Fundatiehuis. Pieter Teyler was a wealthy cloth merchant and banker of Scottish descent, who bequeathed his fortune for the advancement of religion, art, and science. He was a Mennonite and follower of the Scottish Enlightenment.


History


Pieter Teyler van der Hulst


Teylers Museum is named after Pieter Teyler van der Hulst (1702-1778), a wealthy Haarlem silk manufacturer and banker. Living in the time of the Enlightenment, he had a great interest in art and science. Thinking that knowledge could enrich humanity, he built up collections in both areas. In his will, he stipulated that his assets should be placed with a foundation whose objectives included the promotion of art and science.


First museum in the Netherlands


The executors of Teyler's will decided to build the first museum in the Netherlands in which objects of art and science would be united. The books were for study, the physical instruments were used for demonstrations, while the drawings were discussed during art reviews. Fossils and minerals played a part in the public lessons.


Building


Behind Teyler's house in Damstraat, a "book and construction room" was built. This Oval Room was opened to visitors in 1784 and has remained virtually unchanged since then. This makes Teylers Museum the first museum in the Netherlands, which has been open to the public continuously from 1784 and where the collections can be seen in their authentic context. The Teylers Museum building complex covers more than 200 years of construction history. Pieter Teyler van der Hulst, after whom the museum is named, lived in the house at 21 Damstraat. After his death, the Oval Room was opened to the public behind the house in 1784. Fifty years later, the first expansions for the library and art collections took place. At the first centenary in 1878, it was decided to build a new entrance on the Spaarne and the two fossil rooms and instrument room, now the first rooms of the museum. More than a hundred years later, Teylers Museum was due for expansion again. New wing was opened in 1996 with a large exhibition hall, an educational pavilion and a museum café.
